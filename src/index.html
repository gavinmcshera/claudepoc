Getting Started

How to access Claude and find your way around the interface.

  1. Go to claude.ai and sign in with your company email. SSO should log you in automatically.
  2. You'll land on the chat interface. Type a message at the bottom and press Enter to start a conversation.
  3. Your conversation history appears in the left sidebar. Each conversation is separate and independent.
  4. To start fresh, click New Chat. Claude does not carry context between separate conversations.

The interface at a glance

Chat area

The main window where your conversation happens. You type at the bottom, Claude responds above. Attach files using the paperclip icon.

Sidebar

Your conversation history and Projects. Search past conversations and organise work into Projects for ongoing topics.

Model selector

Choose which model to use. Sonnet is fast and good for most tasks. Opus is slower but stronger on complex reasoning.

File attachments

Click the paperclip to attach PDFs, Word docs, Excel files, images, or CSVs. Claude reads the content and works with it.

If you've used ChatGPT The interface works similarly. Main differences are Projects (persistent context across conversations), different model names (Sonnet/Opus instead of GPT-4o), and how Claude handles longer documents. You'll pick it up quickly.

Your first conversation

Start with something real from your day-to-day work. Don't test Claude with trivia. It's most useful when given a genuine task with real context.

Finance starting point
I have a monthly management accounts pack that I present to the board. The pack includes P&L, balance sheet, cash flow, and a narrative commentary. I spend about 3 hours writing the commentary each month. Here is last month's commentary [paste it]. And here is this month's data [attach the file]. Draft this month's commentary in the same style and format. Flag anything that looks unusual compared to last month.
Management starting point
I need to prepare a briefing note for our CEO on the Q3 operational performance of our three business units. I'll give you the KPI dashboards for each unit. For each unit, summarise the top 3 things going well, the top 3 concerns, and any metrics that have moved more than 10% vs prior quarter. Keep the whole thing under 2 pages. [Attach the dashboards]
Quick productivity win
I have a meeting in 30 minutes with the Head of Procurement about consolidating our software vendors. Here are my rough notes [paste them]. Turn these into: 1. A clear agenda with time allocations 2. The 3 key decisions we need to make 3. A list of questions I should ask to understand their constraints

Prompting

The quality of Claude's output depends directly on your input. These patterns will help you get consistently useful results.

1. Give context before the task

Claude knows nothing about you, your company, or your role. The more background you provide, the less time you spend correcting the output.

Without context
Write a variance analysis for Q3.
With context
I'm a Finance Manager at a mid-sized healthcare services company. I report to the CFO and present quarterly variance analysis to the board. Here is our Q3 actuals vs budget [attach file]. Write a variance analysis that covers: - Revenue variance by business unit (we have 3: Consulting, Clinical, and Digital) - The top 5 cost lines with the largest absolute variance - A brief explanation of what likely drove each variance - Recommended management actions where relevant Tone should be professional but direct. The board doesn't want padding. Keep it under 1,500 words.
Why this matters Without context, Claude writes something generic. With context, it writes something you can actually use. The 30 seconds on a better prompt saves 20 minutes of editing.

2. Assign a role

Telling Claude who it should act as shapes the response significantly.

Role examples
"You are a senior management consultant writing a client-facing deliverable." "You are a finance business partner reviewing this budget submission for reasonableness." "You are an internal comms lead drafting an all-hands email about organisational change." "You are a project manager writing a status update for a steering committee." "You are a sceptical CFO reviewing a business case before committing capital."

3. Specify the format

If you have a template or house style, show Claude what it looks like. Claude is very good at matching existing formats.

Template matching
Write a project status update using this exact format: PROJECT: [name] STATUS: [Green / Amber / Red] PERIOD: [date range] SUMMARY [2-3 sentence overview] PROGRESS THIS PERIOD - [bullet points] RISKS & ISSUES | Risk | Impact | Likelihood | Mitigation | NEXT PERIOD PRIORITIES - [bullet points with owners and dates] Here's the raw information to work from: [paste notes]
Controlling length and tone
Write this in 300 words or fewer. Use short paragraphs, no more than 3 sentences each. Tone: direct and professional, no filler language. Audience: C-suite who will skim this in 2 minutes. Lead with the conclusion, not the methodology.

4. Iterate in the same conversation

Your first prompt rarely produces perfection. Stay in the same conversation and give Claude specific feedback.

Useful follow-ups
"Too long. Cut it to half the length. Remove the background section entirely." "Good structure but the tone is too casual. This is going to the audit committee." "The risk section is too vague. For each risk, add a specific data point or example." "Rewrite the executive summary assuming the reader already knows the project context." "This is good. Now create a one-slide version I can use in a deck." "Add a 'so what' after each finding. What should management actually do about it?" "The numbers in paragraph 3 don't match the spreadsheet. Check cells B14 to B18 again."
When to start over If you've gone back and forth many times and the conversation has drifted, start a new chat. Paste the best version so far and give fresh instructions. Long conversations can cause Claude to lose track of earlier guidance.

5. Use Claude for analysis, not just writing

Claude is strong at reviewing documents, finding patterns, challenging assumptions, and structuring thinking.

Challenge a business case
I've attached a business case for a new CRM implementation. The project team is asking for £2.4m over 3 years. Review this as if you were a sceptical CFO. Specifically: - Are the benefits quantified and realistic? Flag any that look inflated. - What costs might be missing? (implementation risk, change management, data migration, ongoing licensing) - Is the payback period calculation sound? - What questions would you ask the project team before approving?
Compare supplier proposals
I've attached two supplier proposals for the same scope of work. Create a comparison table: Scope coverage, Pricing structure, Team composition, Timeline, Key risks, Gaps. Then write a short recommendation (under 200 words) on which is stronger and why. Flag anywhere one supplier has committed to something specific and the other has been vague.
Stress-test assumptions
Here is our 3-year revenue forecast [attach]. The assumptions are: - 8% organic growth per year - 90% client retention - 2 new client wins per quarter at average £500k each Challenge each assumption. What would need to be true for it to hold? What's the downside scenario if each is 20% worse than planned? Build me a sensitivity table.

6. Break complex tasks into steps

Step-by-step approach
Step 1: "Here's our strategic plan [attach]. Read it and confirm you understand the three pillars and the KPIs for each." Step 2: "Now here's our Q3 performance data [attach]. Map the performance data to each pillar and assess whether we're on track." Step 3: "Draft a board paper presenting this assessment. Use the format I'll paste below." Step 4: "The CFO reviewed the draft and wants more emphasis on cash flow. Revise with that focus." Step 5: "Final version is good. Now write a 5-minute verbal briefing I can use to present it."

7. Ready-to-use prompts for common tasks

Meeting preparation

"I have a meeting with [person] about [topic] in 2 hours. Here are my notes [paste]. Organise into an agenda, identify the 3 decisions we need to make, and draft the key questions I should ask."

Email drafting

"Draft an email to [recipient] about [topic]. Key message is [x]. Tone: [professional/firm/collaborative]. Keep it under [x] sentences."

Board paper

"Write a board paper recommending [x]. Include: executive summary, background, options considered, recommended option with rationale, financial impact, risks, and next steps."

Monthly commentary

"Here is this month's P&L [attach]. Write the finance commentary: revenue vs budget, key cost movements, EBITDA bridge, cash position. Match last month's style [attach]."

Presentation narrative

"My slide deck [attach] has weak narrative flow. Review it, suggest a better story arc, and for each slide write 2-3 sentences of speaker notes."

Process documentation

"I'll describe our month-end close process. Turn it into a document with numbered steps, responsible parties, timelines, and dependencies."

Stakeholder briefing

"Brief [stakeholder] on [topic]. They have [x] minutes and care most about [priorities]. Lead with their concerns, keep detail essential."

Contract review

"Review this contract [attach] commercially. Flag: unusual liability clauses, payment terms differing from Net 30, auto-renewal, termination restrictions, scope ambiguity."

Competitor analysis

"Research [competitor]. Summarise: what they do, key clients, positioning, recent news, and where they overlap with our services. Facts, not speculation."

Interview preparation

"I'm interviewing for [role]. Here's the CV [attach] and JD [attach]. Draft 8 questions: 4 technical, 4 cultural fit. Include what a strong answer looks like."

Change communication

"We're [describe change]. Draft three versions: leadership team (strategic), all-staff (clear and reassuring), affected individuals (empathetic and specific)."

Budget challenge

"Here is [team]'s budget submission [attach]. Review as FP&A: flag YoY increases above 5% without justification, headcount changes, discretionary spend, placeholder numbers."

Projects & Files

Projects give Claude persistent context so you don't have to re-explain your world every conversation.

What is a Project?

A Project is a workspace where you upload reference files, set standing instructions, and have multiple conversations sharing the same background. Think of it as a briefing pack that stays available across every chat within that Project.

When to use a Project vs a regular chat

Use a regular chat when...Use a Project when...
One-off question or quick taskOngoing workstream spanning multiple sessions
No background context neededClaude needs your terminology, style, or templates
You won't refer back to thisYou want to build on previous work
Simple drafting or brainstormingRecurring reporting, client work, or team tasks

Setting up a Project

  1. Click Projects in the left sidebar, then New Project.
  2. Give it a clear name (e.g. "Q4 Board Reporting", "Client ABC", "Monthly Close Process").
  3. Add Project Instructions. This is the standing brief Claude references in every conversation within the Project.
  4. Upload reference files: templates, past reports, style guides, org charts, glossaries.
  5. Start a conversation inside the Project. Claude has access to everything you've uploaded.

Writing good Project Instructions

Finance team example
You are helping the Finance team at Inizio with recurring reporting and analysis. Key context: - Fiscal year: January to December - Reporting currency: GBP - Three business units: Consulting, Clinical, and Digital - Board meets quarterly; leadership team meets monthly - ERP: NetSuite When writing commentary or reports: - Professional, concise language. No filler. - Always include variance vs budget and vs prior year - Flag items more than 10% off budget - Use the format from the template files unless told otherwise When working with data files: - Look for anomalies before I ask - If numbers don't add up or look unusual, flag them proactively - Don't round aggressively. Keep to nearest thousand in summaries.
Management / strategy example
You are supporting the Strategy and Transformation team at Inizio. Key context: - Healthcare services company, UK/Ireland/US operations - Strategic plan 2024-2027, three pillars: Growth, Efficiency, People - 12 transformation workstreams, each with a named lead - I report to the COO Preferences: - Structured output: headings, bullets, tables - Lead with "so what" before the detail - When reviewing proposals, be critical. Challenge what might be wrong. - Match the tone and format of the example documents uploaded to this Project

Project ideas

Board reporting

Upload your board pack template, previous commentary, and data formats. Each month, start a new conversation and attach the latest data.

Client engagement

Upload the proposal, SOW, key correspondence, and templates. Use for all work on that client.

Budget cycle

Upload the timetable, submission template, prior year actuals, and guidelines. Use for reviewing submissions and drafting challenges.

Personal productivity

Upload your writing style examples, meeting note format, preferred email structure. Claude adapts to how you work.

File uploads

Supported formats

  • PDFs (reports, contracts, proposals)
  • Word documents (.docx)
  • Excel files (.xlsx) and CSVs
  • PowerPoint decks (.pptx)
  • Images (charts, diagrams, whiteboard photos)

Tips for better results

  • Smaller, focused files beat 200-page documents
  • Scanned PDFs are harder for Claude to read
  • For Excel, make sure data has clear headers
  • Tell Claude which sheet or section to focus on
  • Name files clearly so you can reference them

Excel & PowerPoint

Claude has plugins for Excel and PowerPoint that let you use AI directly inside those applications.

Claude for Excel

  1. Open Excel, go to Insert > Get Add-ins.
  2. Search for "Claude for Excel" and install it.
  3. Sign in with your Claude account.
  4. Claude appears as a sidebar panel. Select data and ask questions.

Excel: formula help

Write a formula
I have dates in A2:A500 and revenue in B2:B500. Write a formula that calculates the average monthly revenue for the last 12 months only. Explain how it works so I can adapt it later.
Debug a formula
This VLOOKUP is returning #N/A for about 30% of the rows but the values exist in the lookup table. What could be causing this? Formula: =VLOOKUP(A2, Sheet2!A:C, 3, FALSE)

Excel: data cleaning

Standardise names
Column C has company names in inconsistent formats: "ACME Ltd", "Acme Limited", "acme ltd.", "ACME LTD". Create a formula or process to standardise them all to proper case with "Ltd" at the end.
Find duplicates
I suspect duplicate invoices in this data. Columns: Invoice Number, Supplier, Date, Amount. Find: exact duplicates (same invoice number), likely duplicates (same supplier + amount within 7 days), and any invoice numbers not matching our format (INV-XXXXXX).

Excel: analysis

Expense analysis
Selected range A1:F200 contains Q3 expense claims. Columns: Employee, Department, Date, Category, Amount, Approved By. Summarise: - Total spend by department - Top 5 categories by value - Any claims over £5,000 - Average claim value by department - Which approver is signing off the most spend
Categorisation
Column D has free-text bank transaction descriptions. Categorise each into: Payroll, Rent, Professional Fees, Software, Travel, Marketing, Utilities, or Other. Put the category in column E. Where uncertain, put "Review" and highlight the row.

Claude for PowerPoint

  1. Open PowerPoint, go to Insert > Get Add-ins.
  2. Search for "Claude for PowerPoint" and install it.
  3. Sign in with your Claude account.
  4. Claude appears as a sidebar to help with content, notes, and structure.

PowerPoint: building decks

From a document
I have a 15-page strategy document. Turn it into a 10-slide executive summary: - Slide 1: Title - Slide 2: Executive summary (key message in 3 bullets) - Slides 3-5: One per strategic pillar with objectives and KPIs - Slides 6-8: Key initiatives with timelines - Slide 9: Investment and expected returns - Slide 10: Next steps and decisions needed No full sentences on slides. Headline + 3-4 bullets per slide.
Speaker notes
For each of these 12 slides, write speaker notes that: - Explain the key point in 2-3 sentences - Include a transition to the next slide - Note likely audience questions Audience is senior leadership. They want implications, not just data.

PowerPoint: improving existing decks

Cut text down
This slide has too much text. Rewrite to follow the 6x6 rule: no more than 6 bullets, no more than 6 words per bullet. Keep the core message. Current slide text: [paste]
Fix the story arc
Here are my slide titles in order: 1. Q3 Results Overview 2. Revenue by Region 3. Market Context 4. Our Strategy 5. Cost Breakdown 6. Headcount Changes 7. Risks 8. Next Steps The narrative feels disjointed. Suggest better ordering and any slides to add or remove to flow from "what happened" to "why" to "what we do about it".
Plugins vs browser The plugins are best for quick, in-context tasks: formula help, text editing, speaker notes. For larger jobs like building a full deck from a document or deep data analysis, the browser gives you more room to iterate.

Guidelines

What's appropriate to use Claude for, and what isn't.

Appropriate use

  • Drafting internal documents, emails, and reports
  • Analysing uploaded data and documents
  • Summarising long documents or meeting notes
  • Reviewing and challenging business cases
  • Creating presentation content and speaker notes
  • Formula help and data work in Excel
  • Brainstorming and structuring ideas
  • Process documentation
  • Research and competitive analysis
  • Meeting preparation

Not appropriate

  • Entering passwords, credentials, or API keys
  • Sharing personal data (employees, clients, patients)
  • Using output in regulated submissions without review
  • Connecting to finance systems of record
  • Letting Claude make decisions on your behalf
  • Sending output to clients without reviewing it
  • Uploading contracts with confidentiality restrictions
  • Assuming numerical calculations are always correct

Accuracy and hallucination

Claude can generate confident-sounding content that is factually wrong. This happens with all large language models. It's most likely when Claude generates facts from memory rather than from a document you've provided.

Lower risk of errors

Summarising, restructuring, and analysing documents you've uploaded. Claude is working from source material you can verify.

Higher risk of errors

Generating specific statistics, regulatory references, case law, or historical data from memory. Always verify independently.

Practical safeguard Ask Claude: "Are you confident in that figure? What's your source?" It will often flag uncertainty if asked. You can also say in your prompt: "If you're not sure about something, say so rather than guessing."
Rule of thumb If you wouldn't paste it into an email to a trusted external consultant, think twice before putting it into Claude.
